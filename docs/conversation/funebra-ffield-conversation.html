Major Strategies for Describing Geometric Shapes

Every geometric primitive can be represented as implicit, explicit, parametric, polygonal/point-set, or procedural/CSG. Each favors different tasks (analysis, rendering, intersections, deformations).

1) Implicit

Surface: f(x,y,z) = 0
Inside:  f(x,y,z) < 0
Outside: f(x,y,z) > 0

Unit sphere: x^2 + y^2 + z^2 - 1 = 0

Conics (2D):
Circle:    x^2 + y^2 - r^2 = 0
Ellipse:   x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 - 1 = 0
Parabola:  y - a x^2 = 0
Hyperbola: x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 - 1 = 0

2) Explicit

One coordinate as a function of others, e.g., y=f(x) or z=f(x,y). Simple to sample, limited for closed surfaces.

3) Parametric

r(u,v) = (x(u,v), y(u,v), z(u,v))

Circle: x=r cosθ, y=r sinθ
Sphere: x=sinφ cosθ, y=sinφ sinθ, z=cosφ
Torus:  x=(R + r cos v) cos u
        y=(R + r cos v) sin u
        z= r sin v

4) Point-set / Mesh

Discrete vertices/edges/faces (OBJ/STL/GLTF) or point clouds — ideal for realtime rendering & 3D printing.

5) Procedural / CSG

Union/intersection/subtraction and field blends; ideal for constructive modeling and SDF pipelines.
